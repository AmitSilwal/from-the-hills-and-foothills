Eighteen Dooars (Frontier Passes)

Eighteen Dooars (Athara Duars)

Meaning of the Term

The word Dooar (Duar) comes from the Hindustani word "Dwar", meaning door or gateway.

Historically, the term referred to the mountain passes connecting the plains of Bengal and Assam with the hills of Bhutan. These passes served as the main routes for trade, administration, and military movement between Bhutan and the plains.

Over time, the name Dooars came to refer not only to the passes themselves but also to the fertile foothill region south of Bhutan where these gateways opened.

Related: Dooars_Geography_Identity

Historical Context

Before British annexation in 1865, the Dooars region formed part of the frontier territory controlled by Bhutan.

Each Dooar was administered by a Bhutanese official known as a Jungpen.

The Jungpen controlled: - the mountain pass - surrounding foothill territory - collection of tribute from plains territories - local administration and frontier security

Because of this system, the Dooars functioned as strategic frontier gateways between Bhutan and the plains of Bengal and Assam.

Bengal Dooars (Western Dooars)

These Dooars were located between the Teesta River and the Sankosh River, covering the frontier of Bengal and Cooch Behar.

  1. Dalimkote
  2. Mynaguri
  3. Chamurchi
  4. Luckee
  5. Buxa
  6. Bhulka
  7. Bara
  8. Goomar
  9. Beepo
  10. Cherrung
  11. Bagk (Chota Bijnee)

These passes connected Bhutan with the plains of Cooch_Behar and northern Bengal.

Assam Dooars (Eastern Dooars)

These were located between the Sankosh River and the Dhansiri River, forming the Bhutan–Assam frontier.

  1. Booree Goomah
  2. Bailing
  3. Shurkolla
  4. Banska
  5. Chappagoorie
  6. Chapkahama
  7. Bijnee

These Dooars connected Bhutan with the plains of Assam.

Strategic Importance

The Dooars passes were historically important because they:

  • served as trade routes between Bhutan and the Indian plains
  • allowed movement of goods such as salt, wool, and agricultural products
  • acted as military corridors during frontier conflicts
  • enabled Bhutan to maintain political influence over frontier territories

British Annexation

The strategic importance of the Dooars eventually led to conflict between Bhutan and British India.

During the Dooar_War_1864, British forces captured many of the frontier passes.

Following the war, the 1865_Treaty_of_Sinchula resulted in the annexation of the Dooars by British India.

After annexation, the region was gradually transformed through:

  • tea plantation expansion
  • forest exploitation
  • colonial settlement
