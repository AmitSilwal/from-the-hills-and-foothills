Dooars

Meaning of the Name

The word Dooars (Duars) comes from the Hindustani word “Dwar”, meaning door or gateway.

These “doors” refer to the mountain passes that connect the plains of India with the Himalayan kingdom of Bhutan and further north to Tibet.

Originally the term referred to the passes themselves, but over time it came to describe the entire fertile belt of land at the foot of the Bhutan hills.

Location

The Dooars form a long narrow strip of land along the foothills of the Eastern Himalayas.

They separate:

  • the plains of Assam and Bengal
  • from the hill kingdom of Bhutan

Extent

Approximate dimensions of the region:

  • Length: about 220 miles
  • Width: between 10 and 30 miles

The region stretches along the base of the Bhutan hills and forms an important frontier zone between the mountains and the plains.

Landscape

Northern Zone

The northern portion of the Dooars lies close to the hills and consists of:

  • uneven and rugged terrain
  • spurs descending from the Himalayan mountains
  • dense forests and thick vegetation

Wildlife historically found in these forests included:

  • elephants
  • rhinoceros
  • tigers
  • deer
  • buffalo
  • large snakes

Southern Zone

The southern part of the Dooars consists of fertile alluvial soil.

Agriculture was possible here and crops grown included:

  • rice
  • cotton
  • tobacco

The region was one of the main sources of tobacco for Bhutan.

Climate

Historically the Dooars were known for an unhealthy climate, particularly in forested areas.

Conditions included:

  • heavy jungle vegetation
  • malaria and other diseases
  • high mortality among people stationed there for long periods

Because of this, soldiers and administrators often found it difficult to remain in the region.

Historical Importance

The Dooars were extremely important because they:

  • controlled access routes between Bhutan and the plains
  • contained fertile agricultural land
  • served as an economic resource for Bhutan
  • became a strategic frontier region between British India and Bhutan

Control of the Dooars eventually became the main cause of conflict between the British and Bhutan, leading to the Dooar War (1864–1865).
