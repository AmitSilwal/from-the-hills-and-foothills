Research Notes

Frontier Relations between Bhutan and the Dooars

The Duars formed the southern frontier of Bhutan and served as the principal gateways between the Himalayan kingdom and the plains of Bengal. For centuries these passes were used for trade, taxation, and political interaction between Bhutanese authorities and the rulers of the plains.

The Duars as Frontier Gateways

The term "Duar" literally means "door" or "pass". The Duars consisted of a series of river valleys and passes connecting Bhutan with the plains of northern Bengal and Assam. Control over these gateways was politically and economically important.

Bhutanese Authority in the Duars

Before British expansion into northern Bengal, Bhutan exercised varying degrees of authority over several Duars. Local chiefs administered these areas and collected revenue from the inhabitants.

British Expansion in Northern Bengal

During the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries the British East India Company gradually extended its influence towards the Himalayan foothills. This brought the British administration into increasing contact and sometimes conflict with Bhutanese frontier authorities.

Rising Frontier Tensions

Disputes over taxation, raids, and jurisdiction became more frequent during the nineteenth century. These tensions eventually culminated in the Anglo-Bhutan War of 1864–1865, after which the Bengal Duars were annexed by the British.
