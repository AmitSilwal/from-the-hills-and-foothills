# Dooars River Valleys

## Overview
The geography of the Dooars is shaped by a series of river valleys formed by Himalayan rivers flowing southward from the Bhutan hills into the plains of northern West Bengal and Assam.

These rivers divide the Dooars into natural geographic belts. Each belt developed distinct settlement patterns, forest landscapes, and later plantation zones.

## Major River Valleys of the Dooars

The Dooars landscape can be understood through the valleys formed between its major rivers.

### Teesta–Torsa Valley
This region forms the western section of the Dooars.

Boundaries:
- West: [[Teesta_River]]
- East: [[Torsa_River]]

Characteristics:
- western Dooars plains
- early settlement zones
- later tea plantation development

---

### Torsa–Jaldhaka Valley
This forms part of the central Dooars.

Boundaries:
- West: [[Torsa_River]]
- East: [[Jaldhaka_River]]

Characteristics:
- fertile floodplains
- forest belts
- plantation expansion during the colonial period

---

### Jaldhaka–Raidak Valley
This valley forms an important section of the central and eastern Dooars.

Boundaries:
- West: [[Jaldhaka_River]]
- East: [[Raidak_River]]

Characteristics:
- dense forests historically
- later plantation zones
- important wildlife habitats

---

### Raidak–Sankosh Valley
This forms the eastern section of the Dooars.

Boundaries:
- West: [[Raidak_River]]
- East: [[Sankosh_River]]

Characteristics:
- eastern Dooars plains
- forest landscapes
- frontier zones near Assam

---

## Geographic Importance

These river valleys shaped the Dooars region by influencing:

- forest distribution
- settlement patterns
- agricultural development
- location of tea plantations

The river valleys also served as natural corridors for movement between the foothills and the plains.

---

## Historical Importance

During the colonial period, many tea estates and settlements were established within these river valleys because of:

- fertile soils
- access to water
- relatively flat terrain

The valleys therefore played a major role in the transformation of the Dooars into a plantation landscape.
