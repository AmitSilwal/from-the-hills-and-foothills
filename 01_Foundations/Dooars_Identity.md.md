# Dooars – Geographical Identity

## Meaning of the Name
- "Dooars" or "Duars" comes from the word **"Dwar"**, meaning **door or gateway**.
- The term originally referred to the **mountain passes connecting the Bengal and Assam plains with the Bhutan hills**.
- Over time the name came to describe the **entire foothill region south of Bhutan** where these passes opened into the plains.

## Location
- Foothill belt along the **southern boundary of Bhutan**.
- Located between the **Teesta River in the west and the Sankosh River in the east**.
- Primarily within modern **Alipurduar and Jalpaiguri districts of West Bengal**, with eastern extensions into **Assam**.
- Part of the larger **Terai–Dooars ecological region** at the base of the eastern Himalayas.

## Physical Features
- **Alluvial floodplains** formed by rivers descending from the Himalayas.
- Historically **dense forests with rich biodiversity**.
- **High rainfall zone** influenced by Himalayan monsoon patterns.
- Numerous **seasonal and perennial rivers** flowing southward from the hills.

## Strategic Importance
- Served as a **frontier zone between Bhutan and the plains of Bengal and Assam**.
- Functioned historically as **trade and movement corridors between the Himalayan region and the plains**.
- The passes were administered by **Bhutanese officials known as Jungpens**.
- Became a **military frontier during British expansion in the nineteenth century**.

## The Dooars Pass System
Historically the region contained **eighteen mountain gateways known as the “Eighteen Dooars”**, which connected Bhutan to the plains.

## Settlement Patterns (Geography → Population Link)
- River systems influenced **early settlement clusters along fertile floodplains**.
- Dense forests limited large-scale settlement before colonial intervention.
- **Forest clearing during the tea plantation expansion** created new labour settlements.
- Plantation zones developed **labour lines and estate-based communities**.
- Frontier geography encouraged **migration from Nepal, Bhutan, and tribal regions of central India**.

## Why Geography Matters
The geography of the Dooars shaped its historical transformation.

It:
- made the region suitable for **tea cultivation**
- enabled **colonial expansion after 1865**
- influenced the **demographic structure of the region**
- transformed the Dooars into a **major plantation frontier of eastern India**
