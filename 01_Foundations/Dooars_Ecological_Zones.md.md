# Dooars Ecological Zones

## Overview
The Dooars region forms part of the eastern Himalayan foothill ecosystem. Its landscape can be understood through a series of ecological zones that extend from the Bhutan hills down to the plains of northern Bengal and Assam.

These zones developed under the influence of climate, rivers, forests, and terrain.

## Himalayan Foothill Zone
The northern edge of the Dooars lies at the base of the Bhutan hills.

Characteristics:
- rugged terrain near the foothills
- numerous mountain streams descending from Bhutan
- dense forests
- wildlife habitats

This zone historically served as the frontier between Bhutan and the plains.

## Forest Belt
Much of the Dooars was historically covered by dense forests forming part of the eastern Himalayan forest ecosystem.

Features:
- sal forests
- riverine grasslands
- rich biodiversity
- elephant and rhinoceros habitats

These forests remained sparsely settled before the colonial period.

## River Valley Zones
Between the major rivers of the region, fertile valleys developed.

These valleys formed natural corridors for:
- movement
- settlement
- agriculture

Major river valleys include those formed by:

- [[Teesta_River]]
- [[Torsa_River]]
- [[Jaldhaka_River]]
- [[Raidak_River]]
- [[Sankosh_River]]

## Plantation and Settlement Zone
After the British annexation of the Dooars in 1865, large areas of forest were cleared to establish tea plantations.

Characteristics:
- plantation estates
- labour settlements
- roads and transport networks
- expanding market centres

This transformed the Dooars from a forest frontier into a plantation landscape.

[[1865_Treaty_of_Sinchula]]

## Importance for Regional History
Understanding these ecological zones helps explain:

- the early isolation of the region
- patterns of settlement
- the expansion of tea plantations
- the environmental transformation of the Dooars during the colonial period.
