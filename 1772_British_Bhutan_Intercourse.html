1772 British–Bhutan Intercourse

Overview

The events of 1772 marked the beginning of direct interaction between the British East India Company and the Kingdom of Bhutan. The conflict arose when Bhutan intervened in the affairs of the neighbouring state of Cooch Behar.

British involvement in this dispute led to the first significant diplomatic and military engagement between the British and Bhutan.

Background

During the eighteenth century the Himalayan frontier was politically unstable.

The state of Cooch Behar was frequently involved in regional conflicts involving Bhutan and neighbouring powers.

In 1772 Bhutanese forces intervened in Cooch Behar and attempted to assert control over the territory.

The ruler of Cooch Behar sought assistance from the British East India Company.

British Intervention

In response to the appeal from Cooch Behar, the British East India Company agreed to provide military assistance.

British forces entered the region and confronted Bhutanese troops.

This intervention forced Bhutan to withdraw from Cooch Behar.

Diplomatic Consequences

Following the conflict, diplomatic negotiations were initiated between the British and Bhutan.

These negotiations eventually led to the dispatch of diplomatic missions to Bhutan and Tibet.
