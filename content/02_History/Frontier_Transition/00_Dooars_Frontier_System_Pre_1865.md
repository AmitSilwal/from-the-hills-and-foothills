# Dooars Frontier System (Before 1865)

## Overview

Before 1865 the Dooars region functioned as a frontier zone between the plains of Bengal and the Himalayan kingdom of Bhutan. The region was controlled by Bhutan and administered through officials stationed at the Duar passes.

This frontier system shaped the political relations between Bhutan and the neighbouring plains territories.

---

## Administrative Control

Bhutan administered the Dooars through frontier officials known as **Jungpens**.

These officials were responsible for:

- supervising frontier territories  
- collecting tribute and taxes  
- regulating trade routes  
- maintaining Bhutanese authority in the Duars

## The Duar Gateway System

The term **“Duar” (or Dooar)** literally means **“door” or “gateway.”** These gateways were the passes that connected the plains of Bengal and Assam with the hills of Bhutan.

Each duar functioned as an entry point through which trade, communication, and political authority passed between the Himalayan foothills and the interior of Bhutan.

Bhutan frontier officials stationed near these passes supervised:

- trade routes between the hills and the plains  
- collection of revenue and duties  
- local administration of the frontier tract

The duars therefore formed a chain of strategic gateways along the Himalayan foothills.

Important duars mentioned in historical records include:

- **Dalimkot Duar**  
- **Chamurchi Duar**  
- **Buxa Duar**  
- **Bishensing Duar**

These gateways controlled access between the Bhutan hills and the forested plains of the Dooars.

### Modern Context

The areas below these historic passes correspond today to regions of northern West Bengal such as:

- **Kalchini**
- **Nagrakata**
- **Falakata**
- **Alipurduar**

After the British annexation of the Dooars in **1865**, these frontier forests were gradually cleared and transformed into tea plantations, settlements, and transport routes.
## Bhutan Frontier Administration

Before British annexation, the Dooars frontier was administered by Bhutan through a network of officials stationed near the passes leading into the hills.

The main authorities in the Bhutan political system included:

- **Deb Raja** – the temporal ruler responsible for political administration  
- **Dharma Raja** – the spiritual head of the state  
- **Penlows** – regional governors controlling large districts  
- **Jungpens** – local frontier administrators responsible for specific areas or passes

Frontier districts in the Dooars were usually supervised by Jungpens or other local officers stationed near the foothills.

In practice these frontier officials often exercised considerable independence, and the central Bhutan government sometimes struggled to control their actions. As a result, raids and disturbances along the frontier were frequent.

### Historical Significance

The weak control exercised by the central Bhutan government over frontier officials was one of the major causes of recurring conflicts with neighbouring territories in the plains.
## Climate and Disease in the Dooars

During the early nineteenth century the Dooars region was covered by dense forests and was widely regarded as an unhealthy frontier zone.

British accounts frequently described the area as suffering from severe **malarial fever**, particularly during the rainy season. The combination of thick forests, marshy ground, and heavy rainfall created conditions in which disease spread easily.

Because of this environment, many soldiers and officials stationed in the Dooars suffered from illness, and long-term settlement in the region remained limited.

### Historical Significance

The unhealthy climate of the Dooars was one of the reasons why the region remained sparsely populated before British annexation.

Only after the British secured control of the Dooars in **1865** and began clearing forests did large-scale settlement and tea plantation development become possible.
## The Eleven Bengal Dooars

The Bengal Dooars referred to the series of gateways along the Himalayan foothills between the **Teesta River** in the west and the **Sankosh River** in the east. These gateways connected the plains of Bengal with the hills of Bhutan.

Historically, eleven principal duars were recognised in the Bengal frontier system.

These were:

1. **Dalimkot Duar**  
2. **Joomerkote Duar**  
3. **Chamurchi Duar**  
4. **Lakshmi Duar**  
5. **Buxa Duar**  
6. **Bala Duar**  
7. **Dalingkot Duar**  
8. **Bishensing Duar**  
9. **Jainti Duar**  
10. **Sidli Duar**  
11. **Bijni Duar**

Each duar represented a gateway through which communication and trade passed between Bhutan and the plains.

### Historical Importance

Control of these duars was strategically important because they provided:

- access routes between Bhutan and the plains
- points for collecting revenue and duties
- military passages through the Himalayan foothills

For this reason, the Dooars became a contested frontier between Bhutan authorities and the British government during the nineteenth century.

### Modern Context

The plains below these historic duars correspond to regions of present-day northern West Bengal and western Assam, including areas such as:

- Kalchini  
- Nagrakata  
- Falakata  
- Alipurduar  

After the British annexation of the Dooars in **1865**, these frontier forests were gradually transformed into tea plantations, settlements, and transport routes.
## Historic Dooars and Modern Geography

The historic Bengal Dooars correspond today to areas in the districts of **Jalpaiguri and Alipurduar in West Bengal**, as well as parts of western Assam. The following table provides a simplified comparison between the historic duars and the modern regions located below them.

| Historic Duar | Approximate Modern Area |
|---------------|-------------------------|
| Dalimkot Duar | Kalimpong foothills / western Dooars frontier |
| Joomerkote Duar | Areas near present-day Nagrakata region |
| Chamurchi Duar | Chamurchi–Samtse border area |
| Lakshmi Duar | Central Dooars foothill zone |
| Buxa Duar | Buxa hills, Kalchini and Alipurduar region |
| Bala Duar | Areas near the Bala River in the eastern Dooars |
| Dalingkot Duar | Frontier hills west of Buxa |
| Bishensing Duar | Eastern Dooars frontier |
| Jainti Duar | Jainti River valley and Buxa foothills |
| Sidli Duar | Areas near present-day Assam border |
| Bijni Duar | Eastern frontier near Assam plains |

### Historical Importance

These duars served as the principal routes through which trade, taxation, and political authority passed between Bhutan and the plains. Control of these gateways was therefore strategically important for both Bhutan and the British.

After the **Treaty of Sinchula in 1865**, the British annexed the Bengal Dooars and gradually transformed the region through forest clearing, tea plantation development, and the construction of transport infrastructure.
## Bengal Dooars from West to East

The Bengal Dooars formed a chain of frontier gateways along the Himalayan foothills between the **Teesta River in the west** and the **Sankosh River in the east**.

Arranged geographically from west to east, the principal Bengal Dooars were:

1. **Dalimkot Duar**  
2. **Joomerkote Duar**  
3. **Chamurchi Duar**  
4. **Lakshmi Duar**  
5. **Buxa Duar**  
6. **Bala Duar**  
7. **Dalingkot Duar**  
8. **Bishensing Duar**  
9. **Jainti Duar**  
10. **Sidli Duar**  
11. **Bijni Duar**

These duars marked the main points where routes descended from the Bhutan hills into the plains of Bengal.

### Geographic Context

The plains below these gateways now correspond to the **Western and Eastern Dooars regions of northern West Bengal**, including areas such as:

- Kalchini  
- Nagrakata  
- Falakata  
- Alipurduar  

These areas later became important centres of tea plantation development after the British annexed the Dooars in **1865**.
