# 1864 Duar War — Campaign Geography

This note describes the geographical flow of the British military campaign during the Duar War (1864–1865).

Related Event:
[[Duar_War_1864_1865.md]]

---

## Campaign Structure

The British advance followed a general pattern from the plains toward the Bhutan foothills.

Plains → Frontier Stockades → Foothill Forts → Mountain Passes

---

## Plains Bases

The campaign began from British bases in the plains:

- [[Jalpaiguri]]
- [[Cooch_Behar]]

These towns served as assembly points for British forces before the invasion of the Dooars.

---

## Frontier Stockades

The first Bhutan frontier positions encountered were:

- [[Maynaguri]]
- [[Dhamonee]]

These stockades were abandoned and occupied by British troops.

---

## Foothill Forts

After securing the plains, the British advanced into the foothills.

Important Bhutan defensive positions included:

- [[Dalimkote]]

The capture of Dalimkote opened the route toward the Bhutan hills.

---

## Strategic Passes (Dooars)

The campaign focused on securing the passes connecting the plains to Bhutan.

Key passes captured by the British were:

- [[Chamurchi]]
- [[Buxa]]
- [[Balia]]

Control of these passes meant control of the frontier gateways.

---

## Eastern Sector Fighting

The most serious resistance occurred further east around:

- [[Dewangiri]]

Bhutanese forces launched counterattacks in this region.

---

## British Operational Base

Later in the campaign British forces assembled at:

- [[Koomrekatta]]

From here further operations toward Dewangiri were prepared.

---

## Strategic Importance

The capture of these locations allowed the British to control the entire Dooars frontier.

This military success led to the signing of:

[[1865_Treaty_of_Sinchula]]

which transferred the Dooars to British India.
