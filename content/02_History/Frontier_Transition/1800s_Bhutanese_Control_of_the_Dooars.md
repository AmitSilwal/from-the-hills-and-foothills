# Dooars

## Meaning of the Name
The word **Dooars (Duars)** comes from the Hindustani word **“Dwar”**, meaning **door or gateway**.

These “doors” refer to the mountain passes connecting the plains of India with Bhutan and further north with Tibet.

Originally the term referred to the passes themselves, but later it came to refer to the fertile plains at the foot of the Bhutan hills.

## Location
The Dooars form a narrow belt along the foothills of the Eastern Himalayas separating the plains of Bengal and Assam from Bhutan.

## Extent
The region extends roughly:

- **Length:** about 220 miles  
- **Width:** between 10 and 30 miles

## Landscape

### Northern Zone
- Rugged terrain
- Spurs descending from the Himalayan mountains
- Dense forests

Wildlife historically included elephants, rhinoceros, deer, tigers, buffalo and large snakes.

### Southern Zone
The southern part consists of fertile soil capable of growing crops such as:

- rice
- cotton
- tobacco

Tobacco from the Dooars was an important supply for Bhutan.

## Climate
The climate of the Dooars was historically considered unhealthy because of dense jungle and malaria. Many people could not remain there for long periods without illness.

## Historical Importance
The Dooars controlled the routes between the plains of India and the kingdom of Bhutan. Because of their fertile land and strategic location they became a major source of conflict between Bhutan and British India, eventually leading to the **Dooar War (1864–1865)**.
