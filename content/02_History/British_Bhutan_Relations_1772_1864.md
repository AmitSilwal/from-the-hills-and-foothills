# British–Bhutan Relations (1772–1864)

## Overview

Relations between British India and Bhutan between 1772 and 1864 were characterized by intermittent diplomacy, frontier disputes, and failed attempts at establishing stable political and commercial relations.

These tensions gradually escalated and ultimately culminated in the **Duar War (1864–1865)**.

---

## Early Political Contact

British interaction with Bhutan began during the **Bhutanese invasion of Cooch Behar in 1772**.

The East India Company intervened militarily to restore the ruler of Cooch Behar, establishing the first direct political contact between the two states.

## Early Diplomatic Engagement

Following the conflict, diplomatic efforts were made to establish peaceful relations and expand trade.

Important developments included:

- The **1774 treaty between Bhutan and the East India Company**
- The **Bogle Mission to Tibet via Bhutan**
- The **Turner Mission to Tibet**

These missions aimed primarily at opening commercial routes between Bengal and Tibet.

## Closure of the Tibetan Trade Route

The Nepal–Tibet War (1791–1792) and subsequent Chinese intervention fundamentally altered regional geopolitics.

China strengthened its control over Tibet and restricted foreign access, effectively ending British hopes of establishing a trade route to Tibet through Bhutan.

## Frontier Disputes and Local Conflicts

Throughout the early nineteenth century, relations between Bhutan and British India were marked by recurring frontier disputes.

Key issues included:

- Bhutanese influence over neighbouring frontier states such as Bijni
- conflicts involving frontier zamindars
- administrative ambiguity in the Dooars region

## Breakdown of Diplomatic Relations

Repeated diplomatic missions failed to produce a stable frontier settlement.

The situation worsened due to:

- Bhutanese raids into frontier territories
- disputes over jurisdiction in the Dooars
- lack of centralized authority within Bhutan's political structure

These tensions eventually led to the **Ashley Eden mission to Bhutan in 1864**, whose failure precipitated the Duar War.

---

## Transition to Armed Conflict

The deterioration of diplomatic relations ultimately resulted in the **Duar War (1864–1865)**.

The war ended with the **Treaty of Sinchula**, through which Bhutan ceded the Dooars to British India.

## Historical Significance

The period from 1772 to 1864 represents the gradual transition from **diplomatic engagement to military confrontation**.

This transition reshaped the political geography of the eastern Himalayan frontier and led to the incorporation of the Dooars into the British colonial system.
