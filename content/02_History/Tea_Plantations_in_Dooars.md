## Overview

The Dooars region became one of the major tea-producing areas of India during the late nineteenth century.

Following the British annexation of the Dooars after the [[1865_Treaty_of_Sinchula]], large areas of forest were cleared to establish tea plantations.

These plantations transformed the region economically, socially, and environmentally.

## Early Development

Tea cultivation in the Dooars began in the late nineteenth century as European planters expanded plantation agriculture from the Darjeeling hills into the foothill plains.

The fertile alluvial soil, heavy rainfall, and humid climate of the region made it highly suitable for tea cultivation.

## Plantation Expansion

During the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, numerous tea estates were established across the Dooars.

Large tracts of forest were cleared and converted into plantation estates.

Plantation development included:

- establishment of tea gardens
- construction of labour lines
- development of transport routes
- creation of plantation settlements

## Labour and Migration

The tea plantations depended on a large labour force.

Workers were recruited from different regions including:

- [[Nepali]] migrants
- Adivasi communities from [[Chota_Nagpur]]
- other labour groups from central and eastern India

These communities formed the core population of plantation settlements.

## Plantation Landscape

The expansion of tea estates transformed the landscape of the Dooars.

Changes included:

- clearing of dense forests
- development of plantation fields
- emergence of plantation villages
- growth of market towns near tea estates

This transformation shaped the modern geography and economy of the region.

## Importance for Regional History

The development of tea plantations explains:

- the economic transformation of the Dooars
- large-scale migration into the region
- the emergence of plantation labour communities
- the development of modern settlements in the Dooars.
