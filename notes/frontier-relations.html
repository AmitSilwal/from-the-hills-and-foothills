Frontier Relations between Bhutan and the Dooars

The Dooars region historically formed a frontier zone between the Himalayan kingdom of Bhutan and the plains of Bengal. For centuries, the passes known as the “duars” served as gateways linking the hill territories with the lowland regions.

These frontier areas were not merely geographical boundaries but zones of interaction involving trade, political influence, and periodic conflict. Bhutan exercised varying degrees of authority over several duars and maintained connections with local chiefs and settlements in the foothill regions.

During the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, British expansion into northern Bengal brought the East India Company into closer contact with Bhutan. Frontier disputes, questions of political control, and issues related to trade routes increasingly shaped relations between the two sides.

These tensions eventually contributed to the conflict known as the Duar War of 1864–1865, after which the Bengal Duars were annexed by British India under the Treaty of Sinchula.

Understanding these frontier dynamics is essential for interpreting the historical geography of the Dooars and the broader political relations between the Himalayan states and the colonial administration in the plains.
