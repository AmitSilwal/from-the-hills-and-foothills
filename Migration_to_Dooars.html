Migration to the Dooars

type: social_change period: colonial region: dooars topic: migration

Overview

The modern population of the Dooars region developed largely through migration during the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.

Following the British annexation of the Dooars after the [[1865_Treaty_of_Sinchula]], large areas of forest were cleared to establish tea plantations. This created a significant demand for labour, leading to large-scale migration into the region.

Over time, these migrant communities formed the core population of plantation settlements and contributed to the ethnic and cultural diversity of the Dooars.

Related: [[Population_Transformation_Dooars]] [[Plantation_Society_Dooars]]

Early Population

Before large-scale migration, the Dooars had a sparse population consisting mainly of indigenous communities.

These included:

  • [[Mechi]]
  • [[Koch]]
  • [[Rabha]]
  • [[Lepcha]]

These communities lived in scattered settlements near forests and river valleys and practiced small-scale agriculture, shifting cultivation, and forest-based livelihoods.

Related: [[Precolonial_Settlement_Dooars]] [[Dooars_Forest_Landscape]]

Migration during Plantation Expansion

The expansion of tea plantations in the late nineteenth century created a demand for labour that could not be met by the small local population.

As a result, plantation companies recruited workers from different regions of the Indian subcontinent.

Major migrant groups included:

  • [[Nepali]] settlers from Nepal and the Himalayan region
  • Adivasi communities from [[Chota_Nagpur]]
  • labourers from central and eastern India

These migrant workers formed the primary labour force of the tea plantations.

Nepali Migration

Nepali migration played a particularly important role in the demographic transformation of the Dooars.

Migration from Nepal increased during the nineteenth century as plantation development expanded across the Himalayan foothills and the Dooars region.

Nepali migrants arrived in two main ways:

Agricultural Settlement

Some Nepali migrants settled as:

  • farmers
  • cattle grazers
  • small landholders

They established villages in foothill areas and forest clearings.

Plantation Labour

Many Nepali migrants also worked in:

  • tea plantations
  • forest clearing
  • road construction

Over time, Nepali communities became an important component of the population of plantation settlements and surrounding rural areas.

Related: [[Nepali]]

Migration from Chota Nagpur

Another major source of plantation labour was the Chota Nagpur plateau.

Adivasi communities were recruited from this region to work in tea plantations.

These included groups such as:

  • [[Oraon]]
  • [[Munda]]
  • [[Santhal]]

Many of these communities were transported to the Dooars through organised labour recruitment systems.

Related: [[Chota_Nagpur]]

Settlement Patterns

Migrants were typically settled within tea estates in residential areas known as labour lines.

These labour settlements formed the social core of plantation life.

Over time, many of these settlements developed into permanent plantation communities.

Related: [[Labour_Lines_Tea_Estates]] [[Tea_Plantations_in_Dooars]]

Social and Cultural Impact

Migration significantly transformed the demographic structure of the Dooars.

The region became home to a diverse population with multiple ethnic, linguistic, and cultural backgrounds.

Different migrant communities brought their own traditions, languages, and cultural practices, contributing to the unique social landscape of the Dooars.

Importance for Regional History

Migration to the Dooars explains:

  • the growth of plantation labour communities
  • the ethnic diversity of the region
  • the development of plantation settlements
  • the transformation of forests into plantation landscapes.
