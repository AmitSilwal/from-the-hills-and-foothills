1783 Turner Mission To Tibet

1783 – Turner Mission to Tibet

Type

Event

Summary

In 1783, the British Government of Bengal organized a second diplomatic mission to Tibet following the earlier mission of George_Bogle.

The mission was led by Captain Samuel Turner, an officer of the Bengal Army. Its primary purpose was to maintain diplomatic relations with Tibetan authorities and to continue British attempts to establish commercial connections with Tibet through the Himalayan frontier.

The mission was also intended to convey the congratulations of the British Government on the recognition of the new incarnation of the Tashi Lama, who had died in 1780.

Journey and Route

The mission departed from Calcutta and travelled north through the plains of Bengal.

The route included:

  • Murshidabad
  • Rangpur
  • Cooch_Behar

From the frontier town of Chichacotta in the Dooars region, the mission entered Bhutan.

The party travelled through Buxa Dooar, following a route similar to that taken earlier by the 1774_Bogle_Mission_to_Tibet.

Delay in Bhutan

Upon reaching Tassisudon (Tashichho Dzong), the mission was delayed for several months while awaiting permission from the Tibetan authorities to proceed further north.

Eventually approval was granted, though with a restriction that only two members of the mission could continue into Tibet.

Arrival in Tibet

Captain Turner and his companions eventually reached Tashilhunpo, the seat of the Tashi Lama.

Turner reported that the Tibetan authorities appeared friendly toward the idea of commercial relations with Bengal. However, he also observed that Tibetan officials were cautious due to the influence of Chinese authority in the region.

Outcome

Although the mission strengthened diplomatic relations, no lasting commercial agreement was achieved.

Later political developments, particularly the Nepal–Tibet War (1791–1792) and increased Chinese control over Tibet, prevented further British attempts to establish trade routes through Bhutan.

Historical Significance

The Turner Mission was important because it:

  • continued British diplomatic engagement with Tibet
  • confirmed British strategic interest in the Himalayan frontier
  • provided valuable information about the political situation in Tibet and Bhutan.

However, it also demonstrated the limitations of British influence in the region.
