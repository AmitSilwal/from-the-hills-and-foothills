Dooars Passes (Duars)

Definition

The term Dooars (Duars) derives from the Hindustani word “door” or “gateway.”

Historically, the Dooars referred to the mountain passes connecting the plains of Bengal and Assam with the kingdom of Bhutan.

These passes served as:

  • frontier gateways into Bhutan
  • trade routes between Tibet, Bhutan, and the plains
  • military entry points
  • administrative frontier zones

Over time, especially under British rule after 1865, the term “Dooars” came to refer to the entire foothill plains region south of Bhutan.

Administrative Context (Pre-1865)

Before British annexation, the Dooars were controlled by Bhutanese authorities.

The passes were administered through local officials known as:

  • Penlows – provincial governors
  • Jungpens – fort governors controlling specific passes and frontier districts

These officials managed trade, tribute collection, and military control of the frontier.

Bengal (Western) Dooars

The Bengal frontier traditionally consisted of eleven Dooar passes connecting Bhutan with the plains of present-day Jalpaiguri and Alipurduar districts.

Commonly recorded passes include:

  1. Buxa Dooar
  2. Chamurchi Dooar
  3. Dalimkot Dooar
  4. Joomerkote Dooar
  5. Bagrakote Dooar
  6. Lakshmi (Laxmi) Dooar
  7. Bhalka / Bhulka Dooar
  8. Daling Dooar
  9. Tendu Dooar
  10. Jainti (Jainty) Dooar
  11. Kumargram Dooar

Passes During the 1864 Duar War

Several Dooar passes became important military routes during the [[1864_Duar_War]].

British forces advanced through these frontier gateways to capture Bhutanese forts and secure the foothill region.

Key passes mentioned in the campaign include:

  • [[Buxa]]
  • [[Chamurchi]]
  • [[Balia]]
  • [[Dalimkote]]

These passes connected the plains of the Dooars with the Bhutan hills and were strategically important for both military movement and frontier control.

Control of these passes allowed the British to dominate the frontier region and ultimately led to the signing of the [[1865_Treaty_of_Sinchula]].

Evidence Status

This list appears in colonial administrative records and gazetteers, though spellings and classifications vary between sources.

Further verification through historical maps and gazetteers is recommended.

Assam (Eastern) Dooars

The Bhutan frontier with Assam consisted of seven principal Dooar passes.

Commonly listed Assam Dooars include:

  1. Bijni Dooar
  2. Sidli Dooar
  3. Chirang Dooar
  4. Ripu Dooar
  5. Chapaguri Dooar
  6. Banska Dooar
  7. Darranga Dooar

These connected Bhutan with the plains of Goalpara and Kamrup regions.

Historical Transformation (1865)

The Dooars region changed fundamentally after the Duar War (1864–1865).

Following the Treaty of Sinchula (1865):

  • Bhutan ceded the Bengal and Assam Dooars to the British
  • the region was incorporated into British India
  • forests were gradually cleared
  • tea plantations expanded across the foothill plains

This marked the beginning of the plantation economy of the Dooars.

Relationship to Modern Dooars Region

Today the term “Dooars” refers to the entire foothill belt south of Bhutan, particularly across:

  • Alipurduar district
  • Jalpaiguri district
  • parts of Darjeeling district

Many modern settlements and tea estates developed in areas historically associated with these frontier gateways.

Research Note

The precise geographical correspondence between some historical Dooar passes and modern locations remains uncertain.

Further investigation using:

  • colonial frontier maps
  • district gazetteers
  • British survey records

may clarify the exact locations of certain passes.
