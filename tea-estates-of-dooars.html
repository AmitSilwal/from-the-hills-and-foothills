Tea Estates of the Dooars

Overview

The Dooars region of northern West Bengal became one of the major tea-producing areas of India during the late nineteenth century.

Following the British annexation of the Dooars after the 1865 Treaty Of Sinchula, large areas of forest were cleared to establish tea plantations. Over time, hundreds of tea estates were established across the region.

These estates transformed the economic, demographic, and social landscape of the Dooars.

Tea Estate Regions in the Dooars

Tea estates in the Dooars are spread across different areas including:

  • Alipurduar region
  • Jalpaiguri region
  • Nagrakata region
  • Banarhat region
  • Kalchini region

Many estates were established along river valleys and foothill zones.

Plantation Landscape

Tea estates in the Dooars typically consist of:

  • tea plantation fields
  • factory buildings
  • labour line settlements
  • forest and river surroundings

These estates function as self-contained plantation settlements.

Importance for Regional History

The network of tea estates explains:

  • the economic transformation of the Dooars
  • migration and labour settlement patterns
  • the development of plantation communities
  • the emergence of modern towns in the region.
