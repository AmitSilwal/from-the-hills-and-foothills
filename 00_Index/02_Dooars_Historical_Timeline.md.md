# Dooars Historical Timeline

This timeline outlines the major historical transitions that shaped the **Dooars region**, tracing its evolution from a Bhutan frontier zone to a colonial plantation landscape and later a modern administrative region.

---

## Structural Phases of Dooars History

1. Bhutan Frontier Period (pre-1865)
2. British Annexation and Administration (1865–1900)
3. Plantation Economy Expansion (1870–1940)
4. Late Colonial Adjustments (1900–1947)
5. Post-Independence Transformation (after 1947)

---

## Timeline of Key Events

---

## Pre-Colonial Period – Frontier System

Before 1865 the Dooars functioned as a **frontier zone between Bhutan and the plains of Bengal and Assam**.

The region consisted of a series of mountain passes (Duars) that served as gateways between the Himalayan kingdom of Bhutan and the lowland territories.

These passes were administered by Bhutanese officials and were important for:

- trade routes between Tibet, Bhutan, and the plains
- frontier administration
- military movement
- tribute collection

## 1772 – Bhutan invasion of Cooch Behar

[[1772_British_Bhutan_Intercourse]]

Bhutanese forces invaded the kingdom of [[Cooch_Behar]] and captured its ruler.  
The East India Company intervened militarily after the Cooch Behar authorities sought assistance.

This event marked the **beginning of direct British political interaction with Bhutan**.

---

## 1774 – Treaty between East India Company and Bhutan

[[1774_Treaty_with_Bhutan]]

Following the conflict over Cooch Behar, a treaty was concluded between the East India Company and Bhutan.

The agreement restored peace and opened diplomatic contact between British India and Bhutan.

---

## 1774–1775 – Bogle Mission to Tibet

[[1774_Bogle_Mission_to_Tibet]]

Governor-General [[Warren_Hastings]] sent a diplomatic mission under [[George_Bogle]] to Tibet through Bhutan.

The mission aimed to establish trade relations between **Bengal and Tibet** and to explore commercial routes across the Himalayan frontier.

Although relations with the **Tashi Lama** were friendly, sustained trade did not develop.

---

## 1783 – Turner Mission to Tibet

[[1783_Turner_Mission_to_Tibet]]

A second British diplomatic mission led by **Captain Samuel Turner** travelled to Tibet through Bhutan.

The mission continued attempts to establish commercial links between Bengal and Tibet.

---

## 1791–1792 – Nepal–Tibet War

[[1791_Nepal_Tibet_War]]

Nepalese forces invaded Tibet, prompting military intervention by the **Qing Empire of China**.

After defeating Nepal, Chinese authorities strengthened control over Tibet and restricted foreign access to the region.

This effectively closed British attempts to establish trade with Tibet through Bhutan.

## Early 19th Century – Frontier Disputes

Relations between Bhutan and British India were marked by frontier disputes involving:

- territorial conflicts
- raids along the frontier
- disputes involving neighbouring zamindars and local rulers.

These tensions gradually increased British interest in the frontier region.

---

## 1815 – Mission of Kishen Kant Bose

[[1815_KishenKant_Bose_Mission]]

A diplomatic mission led by **Kishen Kant Bose** was sent by British authorities to Bhutan to address frontier disputes and gather information about the country.

His account provided one of the early descriptions of Bhutanese administration and society.

---

## 1864–1865 – Duar War

### 1864 — Duar War Begins

British forces launched a military campaign against Bhutan following the failure of the [[Ashley_Eden_Mission_1864]].

Key frontier positions captured included:

- [[Maynaguri]]
- [[Dhamonee]]
- [[Dalimkote]]
- [[Chamurchi]]
- [[Buxa]]

The conflict eventually ended with the [[1865_Treaty_of_Sinchula]].

[[1864_Duar_War]]

The British Government of India fought a war against Bhutan over frontier disputes and political tensions in the Dooars region.

The conflict ended with Bhutan's defeat.

## 1865 – Treaty of Sinchula

Treaty signed between British India and Bhutan after the Duar War.

Bhutan ceded the Bengal and Assam Dooars to British India.

## Late 19th Century – Forest Administration and Plantation Development

[[Tea Plantation Expansion in Dooars]]

After annexation, the British administration reorganized the region through:

- establishment of forest divisions
- forest clearing
- expansion of tea plantations.

The Dooars gradually developed into a major **tea-producing region**.

---

## Late 19th – Early 20th Century – Labour Migration

[[Migration_of_Adivasi_Labour_to_Dooars]]

Large numbers of workers were recruited from regions such as:

- Chota Nagpur plateau
- Central India
- present-day Jharkhand, Odisha, and Bihar.

These migrations led to the formation of plantation labour communities in the Dooars.

---

## Early 20th Century – Consolidation of the Plantation Economy

The tea plantation economy became firmly established.

Transport infrastructure expanded and railway connections linked the Dooars to colonial trade networks.

---

## 1947 – Integration into Independent India

Following Indian independence in **1947**, the Dooars became part of the **Indian Union**.

Administrative changes included:

- integration into the state of West Bengal
- district reorganizations
- continuation of the plantation economy.

---

## Observational Note

The history of the Dooars reflects a transition from:

Himalayan frontier zone  
→ colonial annexation  
→ environmental transformation  
→ plantation economy  
→ modern regional society.
