1865 Treaty of Sinchula

Overview

The Treaty of Sinchula was signed in 1865 between the British Government of India and the Kingdom of Bhutan following the Duar War. The treaty formally ended the conflict and established new territorial arrangements between the two states.

Under this agreement Bhutan ceded the Bengal and Assam Dooars to British India, marking the beginning of British administration in the region.

Background

Relations between Bhutan and British India had been unstable for decades due to disputes along the frontier.

Several issues contributed to tensions:

  • Bhutanese raids into the plains territories.
  • Conflicts with neighbouring frontier states such as Cooch Behar and Bijni.
  • Disputes over tribute and jurisdiction in the Dooars.
  • Failed diplomatic negotiations between the two governments.

These tensions eventually led to the outbreak of the:

1864 Duar War

Terms of the Treaty

The Treaty of Sinchula established several important conditions.

Territorial Changes

Bhutan ceded:

  • the Bengal Dooars
  • the Assam Dooars
  • the hill territory of Dewangiri

to the British Government of India.

Annual Subsidy

In return for these territorial concessions, the British Government agreed to provide Bhutan with an annual subsidy.

Consequences

The treaty had major consequences for the region.

  1. The Dooars came under British colonial administration.
  2. The frontier between Bhutan and British India was reorganized.
  3. Large areas of forest were opened for economic development.
  4. The region was later developed for tea plantation expansion.

Historical Significance

The Treaty of Sinchula represents a major turning point in the history of the Dooars.

It marks the transition of the region from:

Bhutanese frontier territory → British colonial administration.

This transformation later led to the development of tea plantations and new settlement patterns.
