1865 Treaty of Sinchula

Home > History > Frontier Transition > Treaty of Sinchula

The Treaty of Sinchula (1865) was signed between the British Government of India and the Kingdom of Bhutan following the Dooar War (1864–1865). It formally ended the conflict and resulted in the annexation of the Bengal and Assam Duars by the British.

This treaty marked a major turning point in the political history of the Dooars region, initiating British administration and long-term economic transformation.

Overview

The Treaty of Sinchula was signed in 1865 between the British Government of India and the Kingdom of Bhutan following the war.

Under this agreement Bhutan ceded the Bengal and Assam Dooars to British India, marking the beginning of British rule in the region.

Background

Relations between Bhutan and British India had been unstable for decades due to disputes along the frontier.

  • Bhutanese raids into the plains territories
  • Conflicts with frontier states such as Cooch Behar and Bijni
  • Disputes over tribute and jurisdiction
  • Failed diplomatic negotiations

These tensions eventually led to the outbreak of the:

Dooar War (1864–1865)

Terms of the Treaty

Territorial Changes

Bhutan ceded:

  • the Bengal Dooars
  • the Assam Dooars
  • the hill territory of Dewangiri

to the British Government of India.

Annual Subsidy

In return for these territorial concessions, the British Government agreed to provide Bhutan with an annual subsidy.

Consequences

  1. The Dooars came under British colonial administration
  2. The frontier between Bhutan and British India was reorganized
  3. Large areas of forest were opened for economic development
  4. The region later developed into a tea plantation zone

Historical Significance

The Treaty of Sinchula represents a major turning point in the history of the Dooars.

It marks the transition from Bhutanese frontier control to British colonial administration.

This transformation later led to the development of tea plantations and new settlement patterns in the region.
