Dooar War (1864–1865)

Home > History > Frontier Transition > Dooar War

The Dooar War (1864–1865) was a major military conflict between the British Government of India and the Kingdom of Bhutan. Fought over control of the Duars region at the foothills of the Eastern Himalayas, the war resulted in the annexation of the Bengal and Assam Duars by the British.

The immediate cause of the war was the failure of the Ashley Eden Mission (1864), after which British authorities concluded that diplomacy had failed and military action was necessary.

Overview

The Dooar War (1864–1865) was a military conflict between the British Government of India and the Kingdom of Bhutan, resulting in the annexation of the Duars by British India.

Background

Relations between Bhutan and British India were marked by recurring disputes along the frontier.

  • Bhutanese raids into plains territories
  • Disputes with local zamindars
  • Conflicts over frontier states such as Bijni
  • Boundary disputes in the Dooars region

Earlier diplomatic attempts to resolve these tensions were unsuccessful.

Immediate Cause

The immediate cause of the war was the failure of the British diplomatic mission led by:

Ashley Eden Mission (1864)

The mission ended unsuccessfully and significantly increased tensions between the two governments.

Military Campaign

In November 1864, British forces launched military operations along the duar frontier.

Beginning of the Campaign

The campaign began from bases such as Jalpaiguri and Cooch Behar.

On 28 November 1864, troops crossed the Teesta River.

The Bhutan outpost at Gopalgunge was captured without resistance, marking the initial success of the British advance into the Duars.

Capture of Frontier Stockades

  • Maynaguri
  • Dhamonee

These positions were abandoned by Bhutanese forces and secured without major resistance.

Advance into the Foothills

The Bhutanese fort at Dalimkote was captured, securing a key route into the hills.

Control of the Dooar Passes

  • Chamurchi
  • Buxa
  • Balia

Control of these passes gave the British strategic command over the frontier gateways known as the Dooars.

Bhutanese Counterattack

Bhutanese forces launched counterattacks at Dewangiri, where intense fighting took place. The British garrison was temporarily forced to withdraw, making it one of the few setbacks in the campaign.

British Reinforcements

Following this setback, British reinforcements were deployed and military operations reorganized. Strategic positions such as Chamurchi and Koomrekatta were secured, restoring British control over the frontier.

End of the War

The conflict ended with the signing of:

Treaty of Sinchula (1865)

Bhutan ceded the Dooars to British India.

Historical Significance

  1. British control of the Dooars
  2. Establishment of colonial administration
  3. Beginning of plantation economy in the region
